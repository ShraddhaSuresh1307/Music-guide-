Swaras

Carnatic music is built on seven fundamental notes — the swaras. Unlike Western notes which are fixed, each swara (except Sa and Pa) has multiple variants called vikrita swaras.

2 variants:

Never changes.

The Pulse

Tala —
Rhythm & Cycle

Tala is the rhythmic framework of Carnatic music. Unlike a time signature, a tala is a cyclic pattern of beats grouped into sections called angas. Every performance happens inside this cycle.

8 beats
Adi Tala
The most common tala. 4+2+2 grouping. Foundation of Carnatic concerts.
↗
6 beats
Rupaka Tala
2+4 grouping. Very common in kritis and light compositions.
↗
7 beats
Misra Chapu
3+2+2 grouping. A lilting, asymmetric feel used in many popular pieces.
↗
5 beats
Khanda Chapu
2+3 grouping. Energetic and forward-moving. Common in fast-paced pieces.

The Ornament

Gamaka —
The Life of a Note

A note in Carnatic music is never played plain. Gamaka is the system of ornaments — slides, shakes, oscillations — that give each raga its unique character. The same note sounds completely different in different ragas because of gamaka.

Type 01
Kampita
Oscillation — the note wavers between two pitches. The most common gamaka in Carnatic music.
↗
Type 02
Jaru
Slide — gliding smoothly from one note to another, either up or down.
↗
Type 03
Sphurita
A strike — touching a note from above or below, then settling on the target note.
↗
Type 04
Nokku
A press or lean — emphasising a note with a slight push, giving it weight.
↗
Type 05
Odukkal
Squeezing — compressing the pitch slightly below the note, creating a specific tension.
↗

Listening Guide

How to Hear
a Carnatic Song

When you hear a Carnatic performance, what should you listen for? Here is a step-by-step guide to train your ears to identify what's happening.

01

Find the Sruti (Tonic)

The drone in the background is the sruti — the home note everything revolves around. This is always Sa. Find it first.

02

Identify the Raga

Listen for which notes are used, how they're approached, and what emotions they evoke. Is it happy? Melancholic? Intense?

03

Feel the Tala Cycle

Clap along softly. Find the first beat — that's the samam. Count how many beats until it comes back around.

04

Hear the Gamaka

Notice how notes aren't held flat — they oscillate, slide, and breathe. That ornamentation is gamaka. It's what makes the raga come alive.

05

Follow the Structure

Listen for the alapana (no rhythm, pure melody), then the main composition, then the improvised sections (niraval, kalpanaswara).

06

Recognise the Pallavi

The main lyrical phrase that keeps returning — this is the pallavi. Each time it returns, notice how the musician has explored new territory before coming back to it.

Modes —
Seven Flavours

Modes are the seven different scales you get by starting the major scale on each of its seven notes. Each mode has a radically different character — from bright to dark, heroic to haunting.

Notes Together

A chord is three or more notes played at once. Chords create the harmonic foundation of music — the feeling of happiness, sadness, tension, or resolution underneath a melody.

Listening Guide

How to Hear
Any Song

A step-by-step method to listen analytically to any Western song — and understand what you're hearing, not just feel it.

01

Find the Key

Hum the note that feels like "home" at the end of the song. Match it to a piano. That's your tonic — and usually the key name.

02

Major or Minor?

Does it sound bright, happy, resolved? → Major. Dark, sad, tense? → Minor. This tells you the scale the song lives in.

03

Feel the Chord Changes

Listen to the bass and the harmony underneath the melody. Every time it shifts, that's a chord change. How often do they happen?

04

Identify the Progression

Count the chords in one loop. 4 chords repeating? It's probably I–V–vi–IV. 2 chords? Could be I–IV or i–VII. There are patterns everywhere.

05

What Mode or Scale?

Beyond major/minor — does it sound dreamy (Lydian)? Funky (Dorian)? Spanish (Phrygian)? Each mode has a signature flavour.

06

Map the Structure

Intro → Verse → Pre-chorus → Chorus → Bridge → Outro. Notice what changes harmonically between sections. The chorus usually lifts — why?

07

Hear the Intervals

Listen to the melody alone. Is it moving by steps (small, smooth) or leaps (dramatic, wide)? Those distances are intervals — and they create emotion.
